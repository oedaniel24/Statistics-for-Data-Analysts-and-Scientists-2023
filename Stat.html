Video 9 : Chi-squared test of independence

A chi-squared test of independence was carried out to determine if there was a relationship between the curent brand of smartphones being used by people and their preferred brand using the sample data named phone_brand and the following parameters:

Null Hypothesis: No relationship exist

Alternative Hypothesis: A relationship exist

Significance Level: 5%

To recreate this test in R

##loading the dataset
phone_brand <- read_sav("datasets//Example_data//phone_brands.sav")

#converting preferred and current from value to labels
phone_brand$preferred <- as_label(phone_brand$preferred)
phone_brand$current <- as_label(phone_brand$current)


## Creating a contingency table to represent the number of cases that fall into
## each combination of categorical variables
pb_contbl <- table(phone_brand$current, phone_brand$preferred)

#viewing contingency table
pb_contbl
##          
##           Samsung HTC Apple Other
##   Samsung      27  11     4     8
##   HTC           2  25     0     3
##   Apple         3   2    17     8
##   Other         8   2     4    26
#carrying out the chi-square test
chisq.test(pb_contbl)
## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test
## 
## data:  pb_contbl
## X-squared = 131.21, df = 9, p-value < 2.2e-16

Interpretation

With a p-value less than 0.05 i.e (p-value < 2.2e-16) we reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative hypothesis that states that a relationship exist between the brand of smartphone people currently use and their preferred brand.

Video 12: One Sample T-test

One Sample T-test was carried out to see if the average size of herrings have reduced over time from 400grams using the sample data herrings. The test was carried with the following parameters:

Null Hypothesis: Sample mean is equal to 400g

Apparent Hypothesis: Sample mean is less than 400g

Significance Level: 5%

To recreate this test in R

##loading the dataset
herrings <- read_sav("datasets//EXample_data//herrings.sav") 

## Converting variable to a continuous variable
herrings$body_weight<- as.numeric(herrings$body_weight)

To check if the data satisfies all the assumptions needed to carry out the test

# Checking for normality of variable using the Shapiro's test
shapiro.test(herrings$body_weight)
## 
##  Shapiro-Wilk normality test
## 
## data:  herrings$body_weight
## W = 0.95918, p-value = 0.157
# To check for outliers in the dataset
boxplot(herrings$body_weight)

  • With a p-value greater than 0.05 (0.157) in the shapiro test for normality we fail to reject the null hypothesis, confirming normal distribution

To carry out the t-test

t.test(x = herrings$body_weight, mu = 400, alternative = "less",
       conf.level = 0.95)
## 
##  One Sample t-test
## 
## data:  herrings$body_weight
## t = -2.4283, df = 39, p-value = 0.009941
## alternative hypothesis: true mean is less than 400
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##      -Inf 390.6777
## sample estimates:
## mean of x 
##    369.55

With a p-value > 0.05 (p-value = 0.009941), we reject the null hypothesis, favouring the alternative hypothesis that herrings have reduced in size

Video 15: Independent Sample T-test

An independent sample T-test was carried out to see if women and men spend the same amount of money on clothing. 30 male and female respondents were asked their monthly expenditure on clothing, resulting into the clothing_expenses data set. The test was carried out with the following parameters:

Null Hypothesis: Men and women spend the same amount of money on clothing

Alternative Hypothesis: Men and women do not spend the same amount of money on clothing

Significance Level: 5%

To recreate this test with R

##loading the dataset
clothing_expenses <- read_sav("datasets//Example_data//clothing_expenses.sav")
##converting the categorical variable to label from value
clothing_expenses$gender <- as_label(clothing_expenses$gender)

To check if the data satisfies all the assumptions needed to carry out the test

#checking number of categorical groups
clothing_expenses %>% distinct(gender)
## # A tibble: 2  1
##   gender
##   <fct> 
## 1 Female
## 2 Male
#using a box plot to check for outliers
boxplot(clothing_expenses$amount_spent)

#to check for normal distribution using shapiro test
shapiro.test(clothing_expenses$amount_spent)
## 
##  Shapiro-Wilk normality test
## 
## data:  clothing_expenses$amount_spent
## W = 0.81419, p-value = 3.105e-07
#to check for equality of variances using the Levene's test
leveneTest(amount_spent ~ gender, clothing_expenses)
## Levene's Test for Homogeneity of Variance (center = median)
##       Df F value Pr(>F)
## group  1  1.3077 0.2575
##       58
  • The result of Levenes test of equal variance shows a p-value > 0.05 (0.4087) hence we fail to reject the null hypothesis, confirming equal variance in the dataset.
  • The amount_spent variable fails the shapiros test of normality but it is explained that violation of the normality assumption has a little effect on the independent t-test.
  • From the boxplot, an outlier was also identified. To remove this outlier
#determining the interquanrtile range
iqr <- IQR(clothing_expenses$amount_spent)
#determining the 75th percentile
q3 <- quantile(clothing_expenses$amount_spent, probs = 0.75)
#calculating upperbounds
upperbound <- q3 + (1.5*iqr)
#removing outliers from the dataset
clothing_expenses <- clothing_expenses %>% filter(amount_spent <= upperbound)

To carry out the independent T-test

t.test(amount_spent ~ gender, clothing_expenses, var.equal = TRUE)
## 
##  Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  amount_spent by gender
## t = 1.2837, df = 55, p-value = 0.2046
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means between group Female and group Male is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -18.06679  82.46088
## sample estimates:
## mean in group Female   mean in group Male 
##            109.71429             77.51724

Interpretation

With a p-value > 0.05 (p-value = 0.2046) we fail to reject the null hypothesis, hence confirming that there is no statistically significant difference in the average amount spent by men in buying clothes compared to women.

Video 18: Dependent (Paired) Sample T test

A Paired T-test was carried out to test if drinking a single glass of beer affects reaction time. The test was carried out with the following parameters

Null Hypothesis: A single glass of beer does not affect reaction time

Alternative Hypothesis: A single glass of beer does affect reaction time

Significance Level: 5%

To recreate this test in R

#loading the dataset
reaction_times <- read_sav("datasets//Example_data//reaction_times.sav")

To check if the data satisfies all the assumptions needed to carry out the test

#checking for outliers using boxplots
boxplot(reaction_times$reac_1)

boxplot(reaction_times$reac_2)

One outlier is identified in the reac_1 variable, to remove it

iqr <- IQR(reaction_times$reac_1)
#determining the 75th percentile
q3 <- quantile(reaction_times$reac_1, probs = 0.75)
#calculating upperbounds
upperbound <- q3 + (1.5*iqr)
#removing outliers from the dataset
reaction_times <- reaction_times %>% filter(reac_1 <= upperbound)

To carry out the paired t-test

t.test(reaction_times$reac_1, reaction_times$reac_2, alternative = 'two.sided',
       paired = 'TRUE')
## 
##  Paired t-test
## 
## data:  reaction_times$reac_1 and reaction_times$reac_2
## t = -5.2713, df = 28, p-value = 1.321e-05
## alternative hypothesis: true mean difference is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -184.82712  -81.37977
## sample estimates:
## mean difference 
##       -133.1034

Interpretation

With p-value < 0.05 (p-value = 8.29e-05), we fail to reject the null hypothesis in favour of the alternative hypothesis which says that there is a statistically significant difference in reaction times before and after drinking a glass of beer.

Video 24: Simple Linear Regression Analysis Overview

A linear regression model was created to attempt to determine exam scores in the mid-semester exam from the hours spent studying using the study_time.sav dataset which contains records of hour spent studying and exam scores of 20 students sampled. The test was carried out with the following parameters:

Null Hypothesis: The more time you study doesnt mean the higher exam score

Alternative Hypothesis: The more time you study, the higher your exam score

Confidence level: 95%

To recreate this test in R

#import dataset
study_time <-  read_sav("datasets//Example_data//study_time.sav")
attach(study_time)

#To create the linear model
model <- lm(Exam_scores ~ Hours)

To check if the data satisfies all the assumptions needed to carry out the test

#checking for linear relatioship between variables
scatter.smooth(Exam_scores, Hours)

#checking for outliers using a boxplot
boxplot(Exam_scores)

#to check for independence of the observation using the durbin-watson test
durbinWatsonTest(model)
##  lag Autocorrelation D-W Statistic p-value
##    1      0.05214003      1.710846   0.424
##  Alternative hypothesis: rho != 0
#to check for homoscedasticity using the scale-location plot
plot(model, 3)

  • The scatterplot shows a positive linear relationship between the variables
  • Boxplot shows that there are no outliers
  • The Durbin-Watson test yields a p-value of 0.51 and hence we fail to reject the null hypothesis which gives us enough evidence to say the independence assumption is met.
  • The scale-location plot indicates that the model satisfies the homoscedasticity assumption

Video 30: One-way analysis of variance test

A one-way ANOVA test was carried out to test whether the weight of parsely is inflenced byfetilizer and uses data collected by a farmer which contains weight of plants planted with biological fertilizers, chemical fertilizers and no fertilizers at all. With the following parameters:

Null Hypothesis: fertilizer does not affect parsley weight

Apparent Hypothesis: fertilizer does affect parsley weight

Significance level: 5%

To recreate the test in R

#loading the dataset
parsley <-  read_sav("datasets//Example_data//parsley.sav")
parsley$fertilizer <- as_label(parsley$fertilizer)
#descriptive analysis of dataset
summary(parsley)
##       fertilizer     weight     
##  None      :30   Min.   :36.00  
##  Biological:30   1st Qu.:48.00  
##  Chemical  :30   Median :54.00  
##                  Mean   :53.93  
##                  3rd Qu.:61.00  
##                  Max.   :73.00
#descriptive analysis for dataset for each categorical groups
parsley %>% group_by(fertilizer) %>% summarise(count= n(),mean = mean(weight),
                                             sd = sd(weight), min = min(weight),
                                             max = max(weight))
## # A tibble: 3  6
##   fertilizer count  mean    sd   min   max
##   <fct>      <int> <dbl> <dbl> <dbl> <dbl>
## 1 None          30  51.2  7.84    38    68
## 2 Biological    30  53.6  8.86    36    71
## 3 Chemical      30  57.0  7.85    42    73

To check if the data satisfies all the assumptions needed to carry out the test

#checking for outliers
ggplot(parsley, aes(fertilizer, weight)) + geom_boxplot()

#checking for normal disribution for each categorical group
ggplot(parsley, aes(weight, color = fertilizer)) + geom_density() + facet_wrap(~fertilizer)

#checking for homogeneity of variance using Levene's test
leveneTest(weight ~ fertilizer, parsley)
## Levene's Test for Homogeneity of Variance (center = median)
##       Df F value Pr(>F)
## group  2  0.5377  0.586
##       87
  • The boxplot shows the absences of outliers
  • The density plots indicates an approximate normal distribution for each categorical groups and
  • The Levenes test fail to reject the null hypothesis (p-value > 0.05) and hence confirms homogeneity of variances.

The data set satisfies all assumptions to carry out test.

Carrying out the test

parsley.aov <- aov(weight ~ fertilizer, parsley)
summary(parsley.aov)
##             Df Sum Sq Mean Sq F value Pr(>F)  
## fertilizer   2    503  251.43   3.743 0.0276 *
## Residuals   87   5845   67.18                 
## ---
## Signif. codes:  0 '***' 0.001 '**' 0.01 '*' 0.05 '.' 0.1 ' ' 1

Performing a post hoc test

#recreating the tuckey test
parsley.tukey <- TukeyHSD(parsley.aov)
parsley.tukey
##   Tukey multiple comparisons of means
##     95% family-wise confidence level
## 
## Fit: aov(formula = weight ~ fertilizer, data = parsley)
## 
## $fertilizer

## Chemical-Biological 3.333333 -1.7129392  8.379606 0.2618374

Interpretation

There was a statistically significant difference in the weight of plants between groups as determined by the one-way ANOVA test (f(2,87) = 3.743, p = 0.0276) and a tuckey post hoc test revealed that the weight of the parsley plants was statistically significantly lower for plants with no fertilizer than those with chemical fertilizer (p = 0.021). They were no statistically significant differences between plants with biological fertilizers and those with chemical fertilizers (p = 0.262) and the plants with biological fertilizers and no fertilizers at all (p = 0.486)
